Violence and Toxic Stress

  • Shonkoff et al. (2012) find negative effects of toxic stress persist into adulthood.
  • Shern, Blanch, & Steverman (2014) find detectable negative externalities from toxic stress.
  • Warner (2017) suggests that the effect of toxic stress is more severe for women.
  • Our District Safety Advisory Council developed a school safety plan that implicitly acknowledges these effects.
  • Shonkoff et al posit that many adult health issues are manifestations of persistent conditions caused by prolonged exposure to toxic stress beginning in early childhood.

The Data

FCPS Data

  • Student-level Daily Attendance Records
  • Student Demographics
    • race, sex, grade, EL status, FRL, Special Education
    • homeless/highly mobile indicator
    • repeated grade indicator
  • School Identifiers
  • Student level demographic covariates: sex, race, FRL, homeless/highly mobile, retained in grade indicator, EL, SWD, & grade level.
  • Student attendance - Absent, Half-Day Attendance, & Full-Day Attendance; Recoded as Full-Day Present/Not-Full Day Present
  • School Name/ID used to include school level fixed effects.

Crime Data

  • Statute Descriptions
  • Start/End Timestamps
  • Crime Location
  • Used PostGIS (2.3.6) Tiger Geocoder to geocode the location data running PostgreSQL (9.6).
  • Privacy concerns create challenges with accessing and using data.
  • Inconsistencies in address data (e.g., same street address with different zip codes) leads to erroneous geocoding values.
  • Statute descriptions don't always translate to non-law enforcement personnel that easily.

Derived Data

  • Crime Exposure
  • Crime Proximity
  • Attendance Day of Week Indicators
  • Rolling Sum of Crimes
  • Crimes within Geospatial Buffers (e.g., 0.5, 1, & 2mi)
  • Minimum distances within Rolling Sums
  • We assume students would not be exposed to crimes that take place from 0800-1600 Monday-Friday.
  • We used the geodist (Picard, 2010) package in Stata to estimate the distance from students' home addresses to the crime location.
  • Day of week indicators to adjust estimates for students being more likely to be absent on Mondays/Fridays.

Data Preparation

  • Crime data lag attendance data by 1 day.
  • Statutory descriptions reclassified from 309 to 5 distinct values.
  • Standardized the proximity measure to avoid scale-based artifacts.

Methods

Modeling Challenges

  • Absences are rare.
  • Multiple crimes can affect attendance decision simultaneously.
  • Some machine learning algorthims require too many compute resources.
  • The computational cost of complex multilevel models is also prohibitive.
  • Spacetime proximity != social proximity.
  • Limited model fit statistics for Mixed Effects GLM.
  • Multiple spatial relationships per day
  • Additional time dimension per day.
  • When all of the school and other effects are parameterized individually, the matrix is something like 10M x 100
  • LibSVM chokes when using the current data.

Model Fitting


  • Random Forests
  • Generalized Linear Model (GLM)
    • Pooled estimators
      • Complementary Log Log
      • Logit
      • Probit
    • Fixed Effects Logit
    • Mixed Effects Complementary Log Log
  • Talk about how we used these different models to address the challenges from the previous slide:
    • Absences are rare.
    • Multiple crimes can affect attendance decision simultaneously.
    • Multiple time dimensions.
    • Computational cost for estimating models with Mixed Effects is prohibitive.
    • Data are too large for the most popular SVM libraries.

GLM

Model Fit

  • GLM models all had AUC > 0.5
  • Some models lack established GOF measures.
  • Within grade models perform poorly in grades 3-6.
  • Within grade models don't classify any absences in grades 3-6, but improve across grade levels.
  • Mixed Effects models don't have any way to test GOF or quantify how well the model fits.
  • The AUC is primarily a function of correctly classifying attendances.

GLM

Parameter Estimates

  • Estimated relationships are consistently small.
  • Both type of violent crime and proximity have expected relationships.
  • The estimated relationship with the type of crime varies.
  • Small variances in majority of parameter estimates

GLM

Model Diagnostics

  • Greater classification inconsistencies with the rare events.
  • Hosmer-Lemeshow tests indicate good model fit.
  • Models with assymetric sigmoid seem to do slightly better at detecting the rare events.
  • Absences only correctly classified
  • Hosmer-Lemeshow might also be a function of attended days.

Conclusions

Important features

  • Proximity to crime seems to have some relationship with student attendance.
  • The relationship also varies based on the type of violent crime.
  • Male students are more likely to be absent than female students.
  • Economically disadvantaged students are more likely to be absent compared with their counterparts.
  • Reference Hoaglin (2016). We do not control for anything, nor do we hold anything constant.
  • Any results specifically are estimates adjusting for simultaneous variance across the covariate space.

Potential Omitted Variables

  • Cumulative exposure effects in the long-term.
  • Major exogenous shocks.
  • Transportation method.
  • Absence reasons.
  • Absence due to illness and/or family emergency
  • Second bullet is about big events that happen outside of the district but could still affect students.
  • Transportation method - to schools is not always collected with precision (e.g., takes bus/doesn't take bus)

Future Directions

  • Kernel weighted average proximity of crimes.
  • Accumulation effects and varying rolling windows.
  • Additional exploratory work to optimize predictive power of models.
  • Estimating relationship with behavior.
  • Estimating relationship with academic measures.
  • Kernel weighting would be a way that we could collapse the distances into a single row vector to deal with the multiple time dimensions.
